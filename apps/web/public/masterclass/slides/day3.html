609 Letters: The Bureau's Verification Duty

"The 609 letter forces bureaus to verify they have actual, verifiable proof of the information they're reporting — if they can't, they must delete it."
Original Signed Contract
The agreement with your signature authorizing the debt
Complete Payment History
All payments, charges, and account activity
Assignment of Debt
Proof the collector legally owns or is authorized to collect
Balance Calculation
Exact breakdown of how they arrived at the amount owed
When to Use 609 Letters
  • After initial disputes come back "verified"
  • For old accounts where records may be lost
  • When you suspect the creditor lacks documentation
  • For collection accounts with multiple owners
Key Rule: If they can't verify, they must delete the item. Send to all three bureaus separately via certified mail.

Debt Validation: Making Collectors Prove It

1
Send Validation Request
Within 30 days of first contact, send request via certified mail. The clock starts from first contact.
2
All Collection Must Cease
All collection activity stops until validation is provided. They cannot contact you or continue collecting.
3
Collector Responds or Fails
They provide proper proof of debt ownership and amount, or they fail to validate.
4
No Validation = Removal
Cannot legally collect unvalidated debt and must remove it from your credit report entirely.
Proper Validation Requires
1
Original Contract
Signed agreement showing you owe the debt
2
Account History
Complete record of charges and payments
3
Assignment Documents
Proof the collector legally purchased the debt
4
License to Collect
Collector's authority to collect in your state
Strategy: Most debt buyers purchase debts with minimal documentation. Many cannot provide proper validation — giving you leverage to have the item removed.

Escalation: When Disputes Don't Work

CFPB Complaint
File with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau when bureaus or collectors violate your rights.

State Attorney General
Your state AG handles consumer protection complaints. Many states have stronger laws than federal regulations.

Small Claims Court
Sue for FCRA or FDCPA violations. No attorney needed. Filing fees $50–200. Recover statutory damages.
$100–$1,000 per violation + actual damages
Consumer Attorney
Many consumer protection attorneys work on contingency for FCRA/FDCPA cases — no upfront cost to you.

BBB Complaint
File with the Better Business Bureau. Public complaints pressure companies to resolve issues to maintain ratings.
